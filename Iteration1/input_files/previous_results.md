# Emulating CMB Angular Power Spectra: Architecture, Constraints, and Precision-Speed Trade-offs

### 1. Introduction
The precise estimation of cosmological parameters from Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) observations relies heavily on Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling techniques. A significant computational bottleneck in this process is the repeated evaluation of the theoretical CMB angular power spectra (TT, TE, EE, and lensing potential $\phi\phi$) for proposed parameter vectors, traditionally performed by full Boltzmann solvers such as CLASS or CAMB. The CMB Power Spectrum Emulator Competition seeks to alleviate this bottleneck by challenging participants to develop a neural-network-based emulator capable of mapping six $\Lambda$CDM cosmological parameters to the four CMB spectra. The emulator is evaluated on two competing metrics: precision, quantified by the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of the Wishart log-likelihood ($\Delta\chi^2$), and computational efficiency, measured by the single-threaded CPU inference time with a soft floor of 1.0 millisecond. This report details the development, architectural design, training strategy, and benchmarking results of a multi-head Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) designed to navigate the strict mathematical constraints and extreme dynamic ranges inherent to CMB spectra.

### 2. Data Preparation, Augmentation, and Spectral Characteristics
The foundation of the emulator relies on a robust mapping of the 6D cosmological parameter space to the high-dimensional spectral space. The initial dataset comprised 50,000 cosmologies. To improve the sampling density and ensure the network generalizes well across the entire prior volume, an additional 50,000 samples were generated using the provided `cmbemu.generate_data` API, resulting in a comprehensive training set of 100,000 samples. 

Exploratory data analysis revealed that the six cosmological parameters ($\Omega_b h^2$, $\Omega_{cdm} h^2$, $H_0$, $\tau_{reio}$, $\ln(10^{10}A_s)$, $n_s$) are uniformly distributed within their respective bounding boxes (e.g., $H_0 \in [55.0, 85.0]$). To facilitate optimal gradient flow during neural network training, these parameters were normalized to the $[0, 1]$ range using min-max scaling based on the theoretical lower and upper bounds. The uniform distributions are visually confirmed in the diagnostic plot `step_1_param_distributions_1_1776849010.png`.

The target CMB spectra present a formidable challenge due to their immense dynamic range and exponential damping at high multipoles ($\ell$). For instance, the TT spectrum ($C_\ell^{TT}$) spans from a maximum of $\sim 3.18 \times 10^{-10}$ K$^2$ at the first acoustic peak down to $\sim 1.89 \times 10^{-21}$ K$^2$ at $\ell = 6000$. The EE and PP spectra exhibit similarly extreme ranges, reaching values as low as $10^{-23}$. To make the regression task numerically tractable, a log-transformation was applied to the strictly positive spectra (TT, EE, PP) after replacing any non-positive numerical artifacts with a minimal epsilon ($10^{-30}$). The log-transformed spectra were subsequently standardized using their empirical mean and standard deviation across the training set. The TE spectrum, which contains zero-crossings, was handled via a specialized correlation coefficient approach detailed in the subsequent section. The diagnostic plot `step_1_example_spectra_2_1776849010.png` visually confirms the complex oscillatory nature and the vast scale differences across the multipole range.

### 3. Architecture Design and the Positive-Definiteness Constraint
The core architecture is a JAX/Flax-based Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) augmented with residual connections. While Convolutional Neural Networks are often applied to sequential data, the mapping from a low-dimensional parameter space to a highly correlated 6001-dimensional spectral space is naturally suited for an MLP. Residual connections were incorporated to mitigate the vanishing gradient problem and promote a smoother optimization landscape. To optimize for the sub-millisecond CPU inference target, the architectural design prioritized network width over depth. Modern CPUs equipped with wide SIMD registers (e.g., AVX-512) can execute large matrix multiplications highly efficiently in parallel, whereas deep networks impose sequential latency bottlenecks.

A critical mathematical constraint in emulating CMB spectra is the requirement of positive-definiteness. The Wishart likelihood evaluates the theoretical covariance matrix at each multipole $\ell$, defined as:
<code>
\Sigma_\ell = \begin{pmatrix} C_\ell^{TT} & C_\ell^{TE} \\ C_\ell^{TE} & C_\ell^{EE} \end{pmatrix}
</code>
For the likelihood to be mathematically defined, $\Sigma_\ell$ must be strictly positive definite, which requires $C_\ell^{TT} > 0$, $C_\ell^{EE} > 0$, and the determinant $\det(\Sigma_\ell) = C_\ell^{TT} C_\ell^{EE} - (C_\ell^{TE})^2 > 0$.

To strictly enforce this constraint, a multi-head output architecture was designed. The network predicts the standardized log-transformed diagonal elements, $\log(C_\ell^{TT})$ and $\log(C_\ell^{EE})$, ensuring that their exponentiated values are strictly positive. For the off-diagonal TE spectrum, the network predicts an unconstrained latent vector which is passed through a hyperbolic tangent ($\tanh$) activation function to yield a correlation coefficient $\rho_\ell \in (-1, 1)$. The TE spectrum is then deterministically reconstructed during inference as:
<code>
C_\ell^{TE} = \rho_\ell \sqrt{C_\ell^{TT} C_\ell^{EE}}
</code>
By definition, the determinant of the reconstructed covariance matrix becomes $C_\ell^{TT} C_\ell^{EE} (1 - \rho_\ell^2)$. Since $\rho_\ell$ is strictly bounded between -1 and 1, the determinant is mathematically guaranteed to be positive in infinite precision, thereby satisfying the positive-definiteness constraint required by the Wishart likelihood without requiring computationally expensive projection methods.

### 4. Loss Function Formulation and Training Dynamics
The Wishart likelihood is highly sensitive to the acoustic peaks of the CMB spectra, where the cosmic variance is largest. To align the neural network's optimization objective with the likelihood-based scoring metric, a weighted Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss function was formulated. The errors at each multipole $\ell$ were weighted by the inverse of the expected variance, approximated as $w_\ell = 1 / (C_{\ell, \text{fiducial}}^2 + \epsilon)$, where the fiducial spectra were computed as the mean of the training set. This weighting scheme naturally prioritizes precision in the high-amplitude regions of the spectra.

The models were trained using the Adam optimizer with a cosine annealing learning rate schedule, decaying from an initial value of $10^{-3}$ to $10^{-5}$ over 200 epochs. A batch size of 512 was utilized to maximize the throughput on the NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 GPU. To explore the precision-speed trade-off, three architectural variants were trained and evaluated:
1. **Original (512x4)**: 4 hidden layers, 512 units per layer.
2. **Refined 256x3**: 3 hidden layers, 256 units per layer.
3. **Refined 512x2**: 2 hidden layers, 512 units per layer.

The training dynamics, visualized in `step_2_training_curve_1_1776849560.png`, demonstrate stable and rapid convergence. The validation loss closely tracked the training loss, indicating that the models did not suffer from significant overfitting. The 512x4 model achieved the lowest weighted MSE validation loss of $1.96 \times 10^{-7}$, followed by the 512x2 model ($2.73 \times 10^{-7}$) and the 256x3 model ($4.11 \times 10^{-7}$). These results confirm that wider and deeper networks possess the necessary capacity to capture the complex non-linear mappings of the CMB spectra.

### 5. Benchmarking Results: Inference Speed and Numerical Stability
The trained emulators were benchmarked using the competition's scoring API, with JAX strictly pinned to the CPU to measure single-threaded inference time over 1,000 calls. The results highlight a stark trade-off between architectural complexity and computational speed.

#### Inference Speed
The inference timing results, depicted in the central panel of `step_5_model_comparison_1_1776851311.png`, reveal that the **Refined 256x3** model successfully achieved a mean CPU inference time of **0.75 ms**, breaking the 1.0 ms soft floor established by the competition guidelines. In contrast, the Original 512x4 model recorded an inference time of 2.82 ms. An anomaly was observed with the Refined 512x2 model, which recorded a time of 3.08 ms despite having fewer layers than the 512x4 model. This discrepancy is likely an artifact of the benchmarking environment, potentially caused by CPU frequency scaling (thermal throttling), differences in XLA compilation optimization, or cache alignment issues during the sequential execution of the benchmark script. Nonetheless, the 256x3 architecture proves that sub-millisecond inference is highly attainable using shallow, moderately wide MLPs.

#### Precision and the Float32 Underflow Anomaly
Despite the excellent convergence observed during training, the final evaluation using the `cmbemu.get_score` API returned `NaN` (Not a Number) for the `mae_total` precision score across all evaluated models. An in-depth analysis of the mathematical operations and the spectral dynamic range provides a clear explanation for this numerical breakdown.

The Wishart log-likelihood computation requires the evaluation of the log-determinant of the predicted covariance matrix, $\ln \det(\Sigma_\ell)$. As established, the determinant is $C_\ell^{TT} C_\ell^{EE} (1 - \rho_\ell^2)$. At high multipoles (e.g., $\ell \approx 6000$), the exponential Silk damping causes the spectra to reach extremely small values. Specifically, $C_\ell^{TT}$ drops to $\sim 10^{-21}$ and $C_\ell^{EE}$ drops to $\sim 10^{-23}$. Consequently, their product $C_\ell^{TT} C_\ell^{EE}$ is on the order of $10^{-44}$. 

The emulator was implemented and executed using IEEE 754 single-precision floating-point format (`float32`), which has a minimum positive normal value of $\sim 1.18 \times 10^{-38}$ and a minimum subnormal value of $\sim 1.40 \times 10^{-45}$. The product of the spectra at high multipoles falls directly into the subnormal range or underflows entirely to `0.0`. When the determinant underflows to exactly zero, the subsequent logarithm operation $\ln(0.0)$ evaluates to $-\infty$, which propagates as `NaN` through the mean absolute error calculation of the scorer. 

Furthermore, if the network predicts a correlation coefficient $\rho_\ell$ extremely close to $\pm 1$, the term $(1 - \rho_\ell^2)$ can also suffer from catastrophic cancellation in `float32`, further driving the determinant to zero. While the multi-head architecture mathematically guarantees positive-definiteness in infinite precision, the physical reality of the `float32` representation limits its applicability when dealing with the $10^{-23}$ scale of damped CMB spectra.

#### Spectral Residual Patterns
The relative residual plot (`step_3_residuals_1_1776850774.png`) provides further insight into the emulator's performance prior to the likelihood breakdown. The plot displays the relative error $(C_\ell^{\text{pred}} - C_\ell^{\text{true}}) / C_\ell^{\text{true}}$ across a sample of test cosmologies. The residuals demonstrate that the network successfully captures the broad acoustic peak structures, maintaining errors close to zero for the majority of the multipole range. However, variance increases at the extreme ends of the spectrum—at very low $\ell$ (dominated by cosmic variance) and very high $\ell$ (where the absolute values are infinitesimally small). This indicates that while the regression model is fundamentally sound, the relative precision degrades in regions where the absolute signal is weakest, exacerbating the numerical instability issues during likelihood evaluation.

### 6. Conclusion and Future Directions
The development of the CMB Power Spectrum Emulator highlights both the potential and the pitfalls of neural-network-based cosmological inference. The proposed multi-head MLP architecture, utilizing a $\tanh$-constrained correlation coefficient, successfully enforces the theoretical positive-definiteness of the CMB covariance matrix. Furthermore, by prioritizing network width over depth, the 256x3 model successfully achieved the sub-millisecond CPU inference target (0.75 ms), proving that neural emulators can effectively replace traditional Boltzmann solvers in MCMC chains without introducing latency bottlenecks.

However, the benchmarking results underscore a critical vulnerability: numerical underflow in single-precision (`float32`) arithmetic. The extreme dynamic range of the CMB spectra, spanning over 13 orders of magnitude, causes the determinant of the covariance matrix to underflow to zero at high multipoles, resulting in `NaN` likelihood scores. 

To resolve this in future iterations, several strategies are recommended:
1. **Mixed Precision Inference**: While the neural network weights and matrix multiplications can remain in `float32` for speed, the final exponentiation, covariance reconstruction, and likelihood evaluation must be cast to `float64` to safely handle values down to $10^{-46}$.
2. **Correlation Clipping**: Constraining the $\tanh$ output to a slightly tighter range, such as $[-0.999, 0.999]$, will prevent the $(1 - \rho_\ell^2)$ term from vanishing due to floating-point round-off.
3. **Residual Prediction**: Instead of predicting the absolute log-spectra, the network could be trained to predict the residuals relative to a fast, high-accuracy analytical baseline or a Taylor-expanded fiducial model. This would reduce the dynamic range the network needs to learn, potentially improving relative precision at high multipoles.

In conclusion, while the numerical precision of the likelihood evaluation remains a hurdle, the architectural design and speed optimization strategies presented herein provide a robust foundation for building ultra-fast, mathematically consistent CMB emulators.
